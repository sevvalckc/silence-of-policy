Key Finding 1

Reproductive rights & legal discrimination: the largest gaps

Among high-need countries, the share that produced NO actionable commitment on each sub-target:

81%
SDG 5.6 — Reproductive Rights
Largest statistically significant gap (p<0.001). 17 of 21 high-need countries produced no actionable commitment on reproductive health or family planning.
69.6%
SDG 5.1 — Legal Discrimination
Second largest gap (p=0.018). Countries with low Women, Business and the Law scores systematically avoid committing to legal reform in their VNRs.
28.6%
SDG 5.2 — Gender-Based Violence
The only sub-target where the majority of high-need countries DO produce commitments. GBV has become the "safe" domain — consistent with Bumiller (2008).

Key Finding 2

Income inequality does not predict policy silence

Counterintuitively, Q2 countries show the highest gap — not Q4. Kruskal-Wallis p=0.629: no statistically significant regional or income-group variation. Norm diffusion (True & Mintrom, 2001) may drive language production independently of domestic conditions.

Theoretical Framework

Three lenses on policy silence

Policy silence is not a neutral gap — it is an analytically meaningful signal. Three bodies of literature explain why.

Lombardo, Meier & Verloo (2009)
Selective Mainstreaming
Gender equality rhetoric enters policy documents while structurally challenging issues — unpaid care, GBV — are routinely left without actionable language.
Bumiller (2008)
Silence as Political Choice
When governments stay silent on gender issues, it is not an oversight. It reflects what states are — and are not — willing to publicly commit to.
True & Mintrom (2001)
Norm Diffusion
Countries may produce gender equality language because of international normative pressure — not because they face the problem domestically. Norway exemplifies this.
